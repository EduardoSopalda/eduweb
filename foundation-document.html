What This Is

A Scrum-ban System

We run Scrum’s rhythm and roles on a Kanban board. Scrum gives us the predictable heartbeat. Kanban gives us visible flow for work that arrives continuously — which is the reality of a data office.

What We Take from Scrum
Fixed two-week cycles (Sprints)
Planning at the start of each cycle
Named accountabilities — three core roles
Sprint Review at the close of each cycle
Monthly Retrospective to improve the system
What We Take from Kanban
A board with columns — work flows left to right
WIP limit: cap on how much we carry at once
Quality checks built into the flow as work moves
Continuous review in the “In Review” column
Cadences that can be decoupled from sprint boundaries

Where We Diverge from Textbook Scrum — By Design

  • Cadences are decoupled. The retrospective is monthly, not at every sprint boundary. The rhythms do not have to match.
  • No mandatory Daily Scrum. The daily standup is an optional help window, not a required daily sync.
  • Cards are checked continuously in the In Review column, not only at end of sprint. The Sprint Review is Scrum method. Continuous check is Kanban. We keep both.
  • A fourth role — Agile Coach — sits beside the three Scrum accountabilities for one specific, narrow reason. Section 9 explains it.
Section 01

Why This Board Exists

The data office does a lot of work that stays invisible until something breaks. This board makes it visible.

It is not a tracker for managers to watch. It is the place where the team sees what we are carrying, what is next, and what is in our way. One honest view, kept current. We work in two-week cycles. We review and plan every two weeks. We keep a backlog we trust. That rhythm is the point: steady, predictable, and calm enough that good work does not depend on heroics.

The rules that follow are deliberately light

They exist to make the right thing the easy thing, not to add process for its own sake. If a rule ever gets in the way of good work, we change it together. This is written clearly enough that, if it works here, it can run more than the data office.

Section 02

How We Work Together

Three things hold this together:

01

Assume good intent

Everyone here is doing good work with the time and the information they have. We start from that, always.

02

Feedback about the work, not the worker

We talk about what a piece of work needs next, not about who fell short. “This dataset needs a clearer owner before we publish” moves us forward. A judgement about a person does not.

03

Safe to not know

Asking how something works, what a term means, or why we do it a certain way is always welcome. No one is expected to arrive fluent. A question is a contribution, not a confession.

A safe space is not the absence of criticism. It is what makes good criticism possible. When people trust the room, they raise the hard thing early, while it is still cheap to fix.

Section 03

Start Here: Your First Cycle

New to the board? You can dive in from week one, just follow these five steps:

1

Find your work

Open the board and filter to your name. Those cards are yours. That is your week.

2

Read the columns left to right

A card travels: Backlog → Ready → In Progress → Blocked → In Review → Done. Left is waiting, right is finished.

3

Move your card when its status changes

Started something? Move it to In Progress. Finished? In Review, then Done. Do it in the moment, not from memory on Friday.

4

Know the three meetings

An optional daily standup any day you need help. A Sprint Review every other Monday. Sprint Planning the day after on Tuesday.

5

When you are stuck, say so

Mark the card Blocked, bring it to the standup, or ask the Scrum Master. Asking is not failing. A question is a contribution, not a confession.

Section 04

A Few Habits That Help

These habits make keeping the board current cost almost nothing. Some suggestions (not rules):

Review your board at the start of the day

Two minutes with your coffee to see what is yours and what is next. Make it a ritual and you never lose the thread.

Move a card the moment its status changes

Moved in the moment, it costs seconds. Reconstructed from memory on Friday, it costs accuracy.

Mark a card blocked the same day it gets stuck

Blocked means waiting on something outside your control. It is not an admission of failure, it is a signal for help to find you fast.

Glance at your cards before the sprint review

A minute of preparation keeps the meeting short for everyone.

Close what no longer matters

If a card is dead, say so and let it go. A clean board is a kind board.

Assign by consent

A task that arrives without a conversation gets ignored or resented. If you are handing work to another person, ask them first.

The test for a new epic in a nutshell

If the work is large, ongoing, and genuinely distinct from the epics we already have, it earns an epic. If it is a handful of cards that fit somewhere that already exists, it lives there. Every new epic adds a standing commitment and divides the shared hour in the Sprint Review. The call is made together — the Product Owner confirms it fits priority and value, the team agrees at Sprint Planning, and no epic goes live without a named owner.

Where finished documents live: the Hub

The board holds work in motion. Policies, guidelines, and standards belong in the Data Office Hub — the global single point of truth for everything the data office produces. If it is work, it is a card on the board. If it is a settled document, its home is the Hub.

Section 06

How We Classify Work

Every card is one of a few simple types, sits under an epic, and carries a priority. More labels than that and the board starts working for itself instead of for us.

Card Types

Task

Task

A single piece of work with a clear outcome. The everyday unit. Scrum calls it a Story — either word works.

Spike

Spike

A time-boxed investigation before committing. Use it when the honest answer is “we do not know enough yet.”

Impediment

Impediment

Anything blocking work. Tracked as its own card so it is visible and someone owns clearing it.

Epic

Epic

The large container that groups many tasks under one goal. The five live epics are listed in section 5.

Priority

High

Do next

Pull from the top of the backlog. If everything is high, nothing is.

Medium

Do this sprint

Important, but not before what sits above it.

Low

Do soon

Real work, not pressing. Sits in priority order in the backlog.

Estimation in story points

Before starting a card, give it a rough size in story points — a simple number for how big the work feels, not how many hours it will take. Points are quick, shared guesses, not contracts. We use them to learn over time how much we can realistically take on in a sprint. That feeds the improvement loop in section 8.

Section 07

The Flow

A card moves left to right across the board. Here is what each column means, and the two checklists that guard the edges.

Backlog
Everything we know we want to do, ordered by priority. Not yet started.
›
Ready
Refined and clear enough to start. Must meet the Definition of Ready.
›
In Progress
Actively being worked right now. Keep to two cards at most.
›
Blocked
Waiting on something outside the owner’s control. A flag that pulls help, not a parking space.
›
In Review
Done and being checked by a peer or against a quality step.
›
Done
Finished, meets the Definition of Done, and closed.

Work in Progress (WIP) limit

Aim to keep no more than two cards In Progress at any one time. Finishing beats starting. One card carried to Done is worth more than three left half-started.

Definition of Ready — Checklist to Start
It has a clear outcome — we know what done looks like
It has an owner
It is small enough to finish inside a cycle, or broken down until it is
Its known blockers are named
It sits under an epic
Definition of Done — Checklist to Close
The outcome is met
It has been reviewed or validated — not just declared finished
Anything others depend on is updated: document, dataset, handoff
The next person could pick it up without asking you what you meant
Section 08

The Rhythm

Fixed two-week cycles. An optional daily standup for help. Sprint Review every other Monday. Sprint Planning the next day. Retrospective once a month.

Daily Standup
Optional · Any day you need it · 15 minutes

The help window

If you are flowing, skip it. If you are stuck or about to be, this is where you say so. You can invite anyone on the team. If an invitation reaches you, try to join — someone asked because they think you can help.

The Scrum Master sets it up and facilitates on request. Otherwise it runs itself.

Sprint Review
Every other Monday · 1 hour · Close of sprint

A shared look at where every epic stands

The hour is divided equally across epics. No single epic eats the meeting. Each epic’s owner runs their update within the time assigned. Updates follow the work, not the person — if your card sits under someone else’s epic, you give your update during that slot.

The Scrum Master keeps the clock. When the Scrum Master’s own epic comes up, the Agile Coach holds the clock for that slot.

Sprint Planning
Every other Tuesday · 1 hour · Start of sprint

The team agrees what it will take on

Pulled from the top of the backlog. We commit to what is realistic, not what is hoped for. Planning the day after the Review means we start each sprint with the last one’s lessons still fresh.

Every sprint has one short goal: a plain sentence naming what this sprint is really for. Each sprint is named after the Tuesday it begins — for example, Sprint Jun 10.

Monthly Retrospective
Once a month · Short · Honest

What is working, what is getting in the way

The team steps back and asks two questions: what should we keep, and what should we change. This is where the rules in this document get revised. Nothing here is fixed.

The retrospective runs on its own cadence, not at every sprint boundary. Kanban allows it.

The improvement loop

Regularly, and lightly, we look at how long the work actually took against what we expected — not to grade anyone, but so the next plan is wiser than the last. This is where the estimate accuracy signal in section 11 comes from.

Section 09

Who Does What

Scrum uses three core roles. We keep their standard names so this travels cleanly if our board ever merges with another. A fourth role sits beside them for one specific reason.

Sherwin Juco

Owns the what and the why. Decides what is most worth doing, orders the backlog so the top is always the next most valuable thing, and holds final say on priority and the set of epics. Gives the work air cover and removes the obstacles only a leader can remove.

The Product Owner is not always in the room — the team is built not to stall waiting on him. The Scrum Master can make a provisional call when a decision cannot wait, which the Product Owner confirms at Sprint Planning or asynchronously.

Section 10

How the Backlog Stays Honest

A backlog is only useful if it can be trusted.

01

Intake

Every request becomes a card. The board is the only repository for requests. Requests arriving by email come through the Data Office mailbox, managed by Eduardo and Oriol on a rotating two-week basis. New work enters at the bottom, not the top.

02

Ordering

The Product Owner orders the backlog so the top is always the next most valuable thing. When priorities change, the order changes — in the open.

03

Refinement

Once a cycle, before planning, the team spends a short time getting the top of the backlog ready: clarifying outcomes, breaking down anything too big, naming blockers. We refine the top, not the whole list.

04

Pruning

A card that no longer matters is closed, not left to haunt the list. A backlog of two hundred dead cards is not a plan. It is a graveyard.

The test

  • At any moment, the top of the backlog should be the true answer to “what should we do next.” If it is not, the backlog is lying, and we fix it.
Section 11

What We Watch

A few signals, and only a few. Metrics serve the team. The team does not serve the metrics.

Throughput

Cards reaching Done per sprint

Shows our steady pace so planning gets more realistic over time.

Cycle Time

Start to done per card

Rising cycle time is an early warning that something is clogging the flow.

Aging Cards

Cards sitting too long in one column

Especially In Progress or Blocked. An old card is usually a stuck card asking for help.

Estimate Accuracy

Guesses vs what actually happened

Feeds the improvement loop in section 8. Makes the next plan wiser.

All of it is read at the level of the work, never the person. We look at the flow to find where to help, not to rank who is fast. The moment a number is used to judge someone, it stops being useful — because people stop telling it the truth.

Section 12

Handovers & Urgency

Two things every team eventually needs a clear rule for.

Urgency Levels

P1
Drop everything

Something broken or at risk right now

A live data issue, a compliance deadline today, a system down. P1 work jumps the queue and the Scrum Master helps clear the path.

P1 is rare. Calling it when it is not real is how the word loses its meaning.

Cannot wait — minutes
P2
This sprint

Important and time-bound

Can wait days, not minutes. Goes near the top of the current sprint and is done before the sprint closes.

Can wait — days
P3
Soon

Real but not pressing

Enters the backlog in priority order like anything else.

Can wait — next sprint

Anyone can flag something as urgent. The Product Owner, or the Scrum Master acting in his place, confirms the level — so urgency stays a shared judgement and not a volume contest. If everything is P1, nothing is.

Handovers

A handover is not finished until three things are true

The card is current. Status, blockers, and what is left to do are written down, not held in someone’s head.
The receiver has said yes. A handover is an agreement, not a drop. Assign by consent.
Context travels with the work. Links, decisions, and the why live on the card or in the Hub, so the person picking it up does not start from zero.
The simple test: if the person taking over could continue without finding you, the handover is done. If they would have to ask you, it is not.
