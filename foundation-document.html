Section 01

Fundamental Definitions

The distinction between these three concepts is not semantic. It defines the type of governance intervention required for each. Conflating them produces strategies that work for one data type and fail the other two.

📊

Structured Data

Predefined schema. Every field has a name, a type, and a fixed position. The structure exists before the data does.

Inherent. The schema IS the metadata — every field is named and typed by design.

SAP/ERP tables, MDM records, supply chain transaction data, Gold & Silver layer tables in Databricks.

Not the structure itself. The challenge is the operating model: ownership, accountability, and making governance a mandatory delivery step.

📨

Semi-Structured Data

Carries some self-describing properties but does not conform to a rigid schema. Metadata is embedded within the data.

Partial. Some fields are structured (email headers: From, To, Date) but the payload is free-form or variable.

Emails, JSON event logs from manufacturing, application logs, XML regulatory data feeds, SharePoint document metadata.

Extracting and standardising the structural elements that exist, then applying classification to the variable payload.

🖼️

Unstructured Data

No inherent metadata whatsoever. The data does not describe itself. All metadata must be created externally.

None inherent. A photograph knows nothing about itself. Structure must be entirely imposed from outside.

Laboratory photographs, QC voice recordings, process documentation videos, scanned documents without OCR, raw sensor output.

The hardest category. A classification engine must generate metadata at scale, with sufficient reliability to govern against.

Important nuance: assets at the boundary

Many assets sit between categories. A scanned PDF without OCR is unstructured; once OCR is applied and text is extractable, it becomes semi-structured. An email has a structured header and an unstructured body. A SharePoint library carries structured metadata attached to files that may themselves be unstructured. Governance decisions must account for which layer of the asset is being governed at any given point.

1.2 Programme Glossary

The following terms are used throughout this programme. Each has a single, precise definition. They are not interchangeable.

Pool

A logical grouping of related data assets organised by affinity, created before individual asset classification begins. The pool is the initial unit of governance. An owner is assigned to the pool before the details of every asset within it are known.

Container

A governed object within a pool representing a specific data type. Individual assets inherit governance properties from their container: owner, sensitivity tier, retention policy, and purpose tag. Governance operates at container level, not file level.

Purpose Tag

A mandatory metadata field on every container declaring its intended downstream use: ML model training, regulatory reporting, analytics, product, or research. Purpose determines governance treatment and is a legal requirement under GDPR Article 5.

Lineage

Complete traceability of a data asset: where it came from (source system), where it was classified (layer and timestamp), and where it is delivered (target system or consumption pipeline). Tracked at container level, not file level.

HIL — Human in the Loop

A governance practice in which human review is embedded in the AI classification decision cycle at a defined cadence. The HIL cycle ensures the classification engine remains aligned to the current governance policy, not a stale version of it.

RCA — Root Cause Analysis

A structured document identifying why a specific policy violation occurred, distinguishing the proximate cause from contributing factors, and recommending precise interventions. Produced weekly as the primary driver of the HIL feedback loop.

Medallion Architecture

A lakehouse architecture in three layers: Bronze (raw ingestion, no rules), Silver (classified, enriched, policies applied), Gold (governed, AI-ready, published as a data product). Unstructured data enters at Bronze and acquires metadata in transit through Silver.

Knowledge Product

The unstructured data equivalent of a data product. A governed, enriched, and contextually meaningful collection of unstructured assets that has passed through three sequential filter layers and is ready for AI consumption or marketplace publication.

Taxonomy

The classification framework that governs how unstructured data is organised and tagged. Defined before classification scanning begins. Every new data asset is tagged against the taxonomy at the point of ingestion. The governance layer sits on top of the taxonomy as its organisational backbone.

Contextual Semantics

The meaningful context and relationships that AI-generated metadata attaches to an unstructured asset. Contextual semantics make an asset findable, interpretable, and usable by both humans and AI systems. Generated at the knowledge layer of the three filter model.

CLAIRE

Informatica's AI engine within IDMC. Responsible for automated metadata classification, business term matching, data quality rule generation, and anomaly detection. The functional equivalent of the classification engine used on top of Atlan at Meta.

CDGC

Cloud Data Governance Catalog. The Informatica IDMC module containing the business glossary, data catalog, lineage graph, and governance policies. The programme's primary governance tool today.

Technical Role
Scrum: Dev Team Member

Interface between human governance and the automated classification system. Monitors engine output, records and categorises violations, and prepares the technical component of the RCA. Does not make policy decisions.

Automated metadata classification, business term matching, data quality rule generation, and anomaly detection. Always operates under human oversight through the weekly HIL cycle.

Approves domain creation. Manages programme delivery. Signs off on phase activations. Coordinates with DPLs and DGLs across the organisation.

Section 02

The Knowledge Product Model

The unstructured data equivalent of a data product — governed, enriched, and contextually meaningful.

What is a Knowledge Product?

A Knowledge Product is a governed, enriched, and contextually meaningful collection of unstructured data assets that has passed through three mandatory filter layers before it is published or made available for AI consumption. It is not simply a collection of files. It is a curated knowledge asset with verified access rights, confirmed quality, and enriched metadata that makes it findable, usable, and trustworthy.

2.2 The Three Filter Layers

These layers are not parallel checks. They are gates. A failure at any layer stops promotion until the issue is resolved.

1
Access & Restriction
Restricted Non-restrictive Sensitive

Can this data be used at all? Is it restricted by regulation, contractual obligation, or internal policy? Is it sensitive in a way that requires special handling? This layer stops data that should never be consumed from entering the knowledge pipeline.

🚦 Can we use this data?
→
2
Data Quality
Uniqueness Bias Relevance

Is the data of sufficient quality to be trusted? Is it unique or does it duplicate existing assets? Does it carry bias that would distort AI model outputs? Low-quality data that passes Layer 1 is filtered here before it contaminates the knowledge layer.

✅ Is this data good enough?
→
3
Knowledge Layer
Context Semantics Enrichment

Data that passes the first two layers is enriched with metadata using intelligent models. This is how context and semantics are built. The enriched asset becomes findable, contextually meaningful, and ready for AI consumption.

💡 Can this generate real knowledge?

2.4 AI-Generated Metadata: How Tagging Works

✅

HIGH CONFIDENCE Tags

Tags are automatically approved and applied. No human review required. The asset moves forward in the pipeline. The engine learns continuously from positive examples (correct tags) and negative examples (corrections).

⚠️

LOW CONFIDENCE Tags

Tags are flagged for human review. A Data Steward makes the final call. The decision becomes a training example for the next cycle. This is precisely where the weekly HIL cycle operates.

3.2 Medallion Architecture — Bronze → Silver → Gold

Governance flow is always unidirectional. No data is promoted to the next layer without meeting that layer's governance standard.

Layer 01 · Entry
⬇

3.3 The Five Principles That Do Not Change

These principles held at Meta across petabytes of data. They hold at any smaller scale. They are independent of the tooling.

1

Contain before classifying

Create pools by affinity before touching individual assets. A pool with an owner is governable. A million ungrouped files are not.

2

Govern the container, not the file

Individual assets inherit governance from their container. Classifying asset by asset does not scale.

3

Purpose is not optional

Every container declares its intended use. Purpose determines the governance standard, the quality threshold, and the legal basis required under GDPR.

4

Track lineage at container level

Not per individual asset. Origin, classification point, and delivery target are recorded at the moment of container assignment.

5

Human and machine retrained on the same rhythm

The weekly HIL cycle keeps the classification engine aligned to the current policy. Without it, the machine drifts.

Section 04

The Weekly HIL Cycle & RCA Process

Seven days. Fixed rhythm. No exceptions. The mechanism that keeps human intent and machine behaviour aligned.

Why the weekly rhythm was non-negotiable

The data policy was constant. The AI models that applied it were not. They drifted. They encountered edge cases not covered by their training data. They made classification errors that compounded over time. Without regular human calibration, the gap between the intended policy and the policy the engine was actually enforcing grew wider week by week. The HIL cycle was the alignment mechanism: seven days, fixed rhythm, no exceptions.
